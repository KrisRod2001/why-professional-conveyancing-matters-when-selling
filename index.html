Why conveyancing matters

Conveyancing is the legal backbone of a property sale. When selling, your conveyancer ensures that all statutory requirements are met, every disclosure is accurate, every form is completed correctly, and every deadline is met without fail. Without this oversight, a seller can unintentionally breach legal obligations, lose a sale, or face disputes after settlement.

Seller disclosure obligations

In most Australian states, the seller must provide specific disclosures before a buyer signs a contract. These disclosures are designed to give buyers accurate information about the property’s condition, title, zoning, services, and any attached restrictions.

Typical disclosures include:

  • Title particulars
  • Zoning or planning information
  • Council rates and land tax details
  • Service availability (water, electricity, sewerage)
  • Restrictions, easements or covenants
  • Notices from authorities (e.g., road widening, remediation)

Failure to include correct disclosures may allow a buyer to rescind the contract or pursue compensation after settlement.

Documents required before listing

To legally prepare your property for sale, a range of documents must be gathered and reviewed. Your conveyancer typically orders this information on your behalf to ensure accuracy and compliance.

Documents include:

  • Current title search or certificate of title
  • Mortgage information and discharge instructions
  • Local council certificates
  • Water authority statements
  • Owners corporation certificates (for units, apartments, or townhouses)
  • Permits and approvals for renovations or extensions

Preparing these documents early prevents delays once offers start coming in and allows the real estate agent to confidently engage buyers.

Risks of incorrect self-preparation

Some sellers consider preparing documents on their own, but this approach can introduce serious risks. Property law is precise and varies between states, meaning errors are easy to make and costly to fix.

Risk Impact on the sale
Missing disclosures Buyer may cancel the contract or demand compensation
Incorrect contract terms Settlement delays or unenforceable conditions
Failure to follow state requirements Potential legal liability after settlement
Incorrect mortgage discharge process Failed settlement and penalty interest

For most sellers, the cost of professional conveyancing is minor compared to the protections it provides.

Step-by-step overview of the conveyancing process

While each sale is unique, professional conveyancers typically follow a structured process to ensure a seamless experience for sellers.

1. Pre-sale preparation

  • Order title search and certificates
  • Prepare required disclosures
  • Draft the Vendor’s Statement or equivalent
  • Prepare the contract of sale

2. During marketing

  • Respond to buyer legal enquiries
  • Update documents if new information arises
  • Review special conditions requested by buyers

3. Exchange of contracts

  • Verify purchaser details
  • Confirm deposit arrangements
  • Ensure both parties sign correctly

4. Pre-settlement preparation

  • Coordinate with the seller's lender
  • Prepare adjustment statements
  • Sign transfer documents

5. Settlement

  • Ensure funds are transferred
  • Arrange title transfer
  • Confirm mortgage discharge
  • Notify agent to release keys

Managing the mortgage discharge

Most Australian homes are sold while still under mortgage. This means your lender must “discharge” the loan so the title can legally transfer to the buyer. The discharge process involves paperwork and timing that your conveyancer manages on your behalf.

Some lenders require up to 21 days’ notice, so starting early is crucial. If the discharge is not ready by settlement, the entire sale can be delayed—and penalty interest may apply.

Understanding settlement

Settlement is the final step, involving the transfer of funds and legal ownership. It is coordinated electronically in most states and requires accuracy across multiple parties: the seller, buyer, both conveyancers, real estate agent, banks, and government agencies.

Seller responsibilities at settlement include:

  • Confirming adjustment calculations
  • Ensuring mortgage discharge documents are lodged
  • Approving settlement figures
  • Providing authority for final documents

Once completed, keys are released, funds are transferred, and the sale is officially finalised.

How to choose the right conveyancer

Choosing the right conveyancer ensures your sale is handled professionally and without hidden surprises. Look for a provider with strong communication, transparent pricing, and experience handling transactions in your state.

Checklist for choosing a conveyancer

  • Do they offer fixed-fee pricing?
  • Is communication prompt and clear?
  • Do they handle everything from document preparation to settlement?
  • Are they licensed and insured?
